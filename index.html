Working as a Team

A lot of teams struggle during hackathons. You can easily spot the dysfunctional teams, becuase they frequently say things like this...

"I don't know where to begin..."

"I just want to get coding already!"

"I thought you were doing that!"

"I'm out..."

Most of these issues boil down to two things - poor organization and poor management.

Plan your work. Work your plan.

Containing the Scope

Define your problem

Write a problem statement, a brief description of the issue(s) to be addressed. It aids in defining the scope of work to be tackled during the evening by setting some sane "boundaries", and helps ensure that everyone is on the same page.

A good problem statement is made up of 4 components

  • Description - A clear and specific overview of the "current state".
  • Impacts - What exactly is impacted by the problem and how.
  • Success Criteria - How exactly will you know you have solved the problem.
  • Constraints - Impediments to solving the problem that must be overcome.

Be careful not to jump straight to solutions yet!

Organize your team

You need to decide on how you are going to organize your team. This is more important than you may think.

  • Pick a leader that will speak on behalf of the team, settle disputes, remove roadblocks, and set direction.
  • The best coder doesn't necessarily make the best leader.
  • Pick a time/scope wrangler that will keep you on task and on time.
  • One person can serve in multiple roles, so don't limit yourself.
  • Decide how often you should regroup.

Organize your work with Kanban

Kanban is an agile method for visually managing work. This approach presents all participants with a full view of the development process from task definition to delivery, with care taken to balance the demand for work to be done with the available capacity to start new work.

Using your Time Wisely

  • Ask questions and don't be afraid to ask for help.
  • Speak up if you don't agree with something.
  • Do your homework and learn on the fly.
  • Don't use technology {x} for the sake of using it; use the right tool for the job.



Don't be afraid to fail. But fail fast. Move on.

Selling your Product



Presentation Tips

  • Don’t neglect the presentation! It’s just as important as the code you’ve written.
  • Slides are better than nothing. Talking is better than just slides. A demo is a million times better than both.
  • Practice makes perfect. Set aside at least one hour for your whole team to practice presenting.
  • Know your audience - Your company doesn’t care that you used Super-Fancy-Widget. They care that it solves their problems.
  • Good feature ideas you haven’t implemented yet? Write it down! (Hint: Look at your Kanban board for features you dropped)
  • Be Honest - all the features aren’t there, or your app is broken because you ran out of time? Tell them!


Kick-starting this Thing

Pick your tools

Whatever you do, don't try to do it from scratch unless you really know what you are doing. Even then, you'll still be pressed for time. You need to be focused on solving your business problems, not reinventing the wheel. You can get up and running quickly, easily, and securely, by using common tools and frameworks.

No idea where to start? No idea which is better at {thing}? Ask your friendly neighborhood SME or Mentor! Or, take a look at what the broader community thinks...

Find a framework for: Java, Python, PHP, Perl, JavaScript, HTML/CSS, etc.



Challenge 1

Accessability information

Information about places that are accessable can sometimes be difficult to find. For individuals with disabilities, details like door widths, bathroom accessability and table heights can be crutial drivers in deciding which places they may consider visiting. Develop a web or mobile app that will help incentivize people - with or without disabilities - to provide more structured information on accessable spaces.

Challenge 2

Inspiring Stem education

The demand for computer science graduates will continue to grow in the US over the next 5 years, vastly outpacing the supply of CS grads. Develop a simple web based game that helps introduce kids to computer science. The game must be something that can be experienced by a group of kids working together and must have a competetive element for them to compete in real time against other teams.
